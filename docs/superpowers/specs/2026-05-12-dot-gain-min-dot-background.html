How we got to the Dot Gain & Min Dot design

A condensed walk-through of the brainstorming conversation that produced the design spec. Captures the decisions taken and the reasoning behind them so future readers can understand why the spec looks the way it does — and so that revisiting the design later doesn't have to rehash settled ground.

See the design spec

The starting point

The visualizer's right side (the solid-tone reference) was using pure Murray–Davies / Neugebauer math with raw slider coverage values — no TVI, no dot gain modeled. That's mathematically clean but represents a hypothetical zero-gain press, not anything real. The proposal: add a dot gain function and a min dot function so the visualizer can model real press behaviour and let users compare what they design against to what their actual press will produce.

Decisions, in order

1. Scope: single global knobs, not per-channel or presets

Four options on the table: single global knobs, per-channel CMYK, paper-type presets (Coated / Uncoated / Newsprint / Flexo), or full custom TVI curves.

Chose: single global knobs.

With a wrinkle — the user wanted "something a little more in-depth than a fixed-shape curve," specifically pointing to perimeter-driven gain (so the curve emerges from geometry rather than being fitted) and min dot with a printed companion (because processes like flexo make the smallest reliable dot print far larger than its file value). Per-channel and presets noted as future possible additions.

2. Min dot is a hard step (plate physics), not a soft transition

Should the min-dot floor be a hard step (drops to 0 below threshold) or a soft fade?

Chose: hard step.

Min dot is a function of plate material and imaging process — features smaller than the plate's resolution cannot be produced at all. It's a physical impossibility, not a quality issue. So the model uses if c < minDot → 0.

3. "Min Dot Printed" stays as a third explicit knob, not derived

Earlier brainstorming considered dropping the explicit printedMin slider and letting the model derive what the min dot prints as from the dot gain knob automatically.

Chose: keep it as a third input.

Press-people think in these terms directly ("our 3% min dot prints at 18% on this stock"), and the difference between coated offset (low bloom) and flexo (high bloom) is large enough that one coupled knob can't honestly capture both. Three explicit inputs, kept tidy by hiding them behind a disclosure.

4. Defaults match coated / GRACoL on first load

Should the visualizer load with zero gain (clean math, unrealistic) or with realistic coated-press defaults baked in?

Chose: coated GRACoL defaults — Dot Gain 18%, Min Dot 3%, Min Dot Printed 5%.

Most graphic-design software (Photoshop, Illustrator) assumes ~18% midtone gain via the embedded ICC profile when soft-proofing CMYK. Making the visualizer match that assumption on first load means a designer's expectations from Adobe carry over directly, rather than the visualizer looking conspicuously too clean.

5. Right side stays a constant GRACoL coated reference

Should the gain knobs affect both sides of the visualizer, or only the halftone view?

Chose: gain affects the halftone (left) only. The solid-tone (right) is always the GRACoL coated reference — "what the designer sees in Adobe soft-proof."

This turned the side-by-side into a much sharper teaching tool: the left side shows what physically prints on the user's stock, the right shows what they designed against. At coated defaults the two halves roughly agree; as the user dials toward flexo or newsprint, they visibly diverge. The visualizer becomes a literal demonstration of "this is what you designed vs. what you'll get on press."

6. GRACoL reference uses hardcoded TVI tables, not an ICC profile

For the right side to actually match Adobe soft-proof, we need to apply GRACoL's TVI somewhere. Two ways: (a) hardcoded per-channel TVI numbers fed into the existing Neugebauer math, or (b) load a GRACoL ICC profile and run a CMYK → RGB lookup.

Chose: hardcoded TVI + existing Neugebauer.

ICC profile lookup gives a near-exact Adobe match but adds ~200 LOC, a ~50–500 KB profile blob, and a black-box LUT-interp step that hides the math. For a visualizer whose pedagogical pitch is "see the math behind halftones," exposing the GRACoL TVI as four published numbers (C +12, M +14, Y +13, K +18 at midtone) plus the existing Neugebauer code is dramatically more readable. ~95% as accurate, ~10% as much code.

7. UI: disclosure widget with three number-entry boxes

Three sliders or three number inputs? Visible always or hidden by default?

Chose: three <input type="number"> boxes behind a <details> disclosure labelled "Dot Gain," closed by default.

These are values that are typed, not dialed (you know your stock's min dot; you don't experiment with it the way you do ink coverage). Hiding them behind a disclosure keeps the visualizer's first impression unchanged while making the controls discoverable.

The physics: why flexo small dots blow up

This wasn't a decision so much as a shared diagnosis that shaped the model. A perimeter-spread model alone gives gain ∝ √area, which under-predicts flexo's small-dot behaviour by a wide margin (real flexo: a 3% file dot often prints around 15–18% — a 500% relative gain). Several mechanisms stack at the small end:

  • Ink film thickness + viscosity — flexo wet-film is ~3–8 μm at low viscosity; the anilox dumps a volume of ink onto each plate dot that's roughly volume-proportional, not area-proportional. Small dots get a tall ink "dome" that squashes outward on impression.
  • Elastomeric plate squish — photopolymer/rubber plates deform under impression. Smaller dots have more boundary relative to centroid, so they squash proportionally more.
  • Plate exposure gain — UV scatter during plate exposure makes small features come off the imaging step already larger than their digital area. For a 3% file dot, the plate dot might be 7–8% before any ink even touches it.
  • Anilox cell over-supply — fixed-volume cells release ink whose volume is matched to cell size, not to the tiny dot it lands on. Excess wicks outward.
  • Substrate absorption / capillary spread — low-viscosity ink wicks into corrugated, kraft, or label stock substrates. The wicking distance is a function of substrate, not of dot size, so a 3% dot gets the same wicking ring as a 50% dot — a much larger relative share at the small end.
  • Surface tension on hydrophobic substrates — for tiny features, the ink minimizes surface area, which can mean spreading laterally past the plate footprint before drying.

These all stack at the small end of the curve and barely matter past midtones. That's why the model needs two components: a perimeter-driven midtone spread (the spreadTerm), and a decaying small-end bloom anchored at min dot (the bloomTerm). Together they reproduce flexo-style gain curves with three intuitive knobs.

The resulting model (quick recap)

Three user knobs feed a two-component additive gain:

InputAnchorsMaps to
Dot Gain Extra coverage at midtone (c = 0.5) spreadTerm(c) = dotGain · 4 · c · (1 − c)
Min Dot File floor: below this, prints as 0 Hard step before the gain pipeline; also anchors the bloom decay
Min Dot Printed What a min-dot value deposits on paper bloomTerm(c) = (minDotPrinted − minDot) · decay(c)

The shape bell(c) = 4·c·(1−c) peaks at midtone and falls to 0 at the extremes. The shape decay(c) is 1 at c = minDot and falls to 0 by midtone. Adding the two components recovers the three anchor points exactly: (minDot, minDotPrinted), (0.5, 0.5 + dotGain), and (1.0, 1.0). Right side ignores all three knobs and applies a fixed per-channel GRACoL coated TVI (C +12%, M +14%, Y +13%, K +18% at midtone) before the existing Neugebauer mix.

What was deliberately left out

  • Per-channel CMYK gain values (single global only in v1)
  • Press-type presets (Coated / Uncoated / Newsprint / Flexo dropdown)
  • Yule–Nielsen n-factor in Neugebauer (kept pure Murray–Davies)
  • Persisting values across reloads (defaults every time)
  • "Right side mirrors press" advanced toggle (mentioned but deferred)
  • ICC profile transformation for the right side

All of these are tractable additions; none of them are required for the visualizer to demonstrate what it's supposed to demonstrate. They can be layered on later as discrete follow-ups.
